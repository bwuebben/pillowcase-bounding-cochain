\title[Instanton homology of $(-2,3,q)$ pretzels and pillowcase cochains]
{The instanton homology of the $(-2,3,q)$ pretzel knots\\ and computed bounding cochains in the pillowcase}

\author{Bernd J. Wuebben}
\date{July 17, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We prove that the reduced singular instanton knot homology of the pretzel knots $P(-2,3,q)$ has rank
$q+2$ for every odd $q\ge3$: the Alexander polynomials of the family, computed in closed form by a
skein recursion (they are Hironaka's Lehmer-like polynomials, with all coefficients in $\{0,\pm1\}$),
give the lower bound $q+2$, and Manion's closed-form reduced Khovanov homology gives the matching upper
bound. We then compare this against the pillowcase (symplectic) side of the knot Atiyah--Floer program.
Working in the immersed-curve combinatorial model of Herald--Kirk and Smith, we reconstruct the
pillowcase Lagrangians of the natural tangle decomposition of the family and compute the naive
Lagrangian--Floer homology of its members through $q=13$. The outcome is a sharp experimental law: the
naive rank differs from $\rk\Inat$ by \emph{exactly one differential},
\[
   \text{naive rank} \;-\; \rk\Inat \;=\; 2\,\sgn(\det K - 3),
\]
vanishing for the torus member $q=3$ and changing direction as the determinant $\det=|q-6|$ crosses $3$
(equivalently, as binary-dihedral traceless characters appear). Finally we compute the bounding
cochains --- the correction terms conjectured by Cazassus--Herald--Kirk--Kotelskiy --- that repair the
deficiency: for $q=5$ a unique two-crossing cochain acting through an immersed quadrilateral, for $q=7$
a unique single crossing acting through a triangle (each \emph{cancelling} a bigon and raising the rank
by $2$), and for $q=11$ single-crossing cochains acting in the \emph{opposite} direction (creating a
differential and lowering the rank by $2$). These are, to our knowledge, the first explicitly computed
nonzero bounding cochains in pillowcase Floer theory, and they realize both directions of the
conjectured correction within a single family, with a marked rigidity asymmetry: cancellation admits a
unique minimal cochain, creation many. We separate throughout what is proved unconditionally, what is
computed within the combinatorial model, and what remains conjectural.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Two homologies for one knot}
Let $K\subset S^3$ be a knot. On the gauge-theoretic side sits the reduced singular instanton knot
homology $\Inat(K)$ of Kronheimer--Mrowka \cite{KM1,KM2}, whose graded Euler characteristic is the
Alexander polynomial \cite{Lim,KM1} and which receives a spectral sequence from reduced Khovanov
homology \cite{KM2}; hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:squeeze}
   \ell(K):=\textstyle\sum_i |a_i(\Delta_K)| \;\le\; \rk \Inat(K) \;\le\; \dim \Kh_r(K)=:u(K).
\end{equation}
On the symplectic side sits the pillowcase program of Hedden--Herald--Kirk \cite{HHK1,HHK2}. Present
$K=(B^3,T_1)\cup_{(S^2,4)}(B^3,T_2)$ as a union of two tangles. The traceless $\SU$ character variety
of the four-punctured sphere is the \emph{pillowcase}
\[
   \pill=(\R/2\pi\Z)^2/\iota,\qquad \iota(\gamma,\theta)=(-\gamma,-\theta),
\]
a two-sphere with four $\Z/2$ orbifold points and symplectic form $d\gamma\wedge d\theta$ on the smooth
locus $\pill^*$ \cite{Lin,HHK1}. Each tangle determines an immersed Lagrangian
$\Rp_{\pi_i}(T_i)\iss\pill^*$, the intersections $\Rp_{\pi_1}(T_1)\cap\Rp_{\pi_2}(T_2)$ are the
traceless representations of the knot group, and one forms their Lagrangian--Floer homology --- an
intended Atiyah--Floer counterpart of $\Inat(K)$.

This naive Floer homology is \emph{not} a knot invariant: figure-eight bubbling at the curves'
self-intersections spoils invariance under tangle moves. Cazassus, Herald, Kirk and Kotelskiy
\cite{CHKK} conjectured the repair: each tangle carries a \emph{bounding cochain}
$\bc\in\CF(\Rp(T),\Rp(T))$, an $\F_2$-combination of the self-intersection points satisfying the
Maurer--Cartan equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MC}
   \sum_{k\ge0}\mu^k(\bc,\dots,\bc)=0
\end{equation}
($\mu^k$ counts immersed $(k{+}1)$-gons), and the $\bc$-deformed Floer homology
$\HF\big((\Rp(T_1),\bc_1),(\Rp(T_2),\bc_2)\big)$ is conjecturally the knot invariant, equal to
$\Inat(K)$ \cite[Conj.~1.1]{CHKK}. Before this work, one bounding cochain had been exhibited (on the
earring tangle of $T(4,5)$ \cite[\S10]{CHKK}), one had been proven nonzero without being computed (the
Conway-sum tangle of $P(-2,3,5)$, Smith \cite[Thm.~5.9]{Smith}), and none had been computed.

This paper studies both sides for the pretzel family
\begin{equation}\label{eq:family}
   K_q := P(-2,3,q)=\operatorname{num}\big(Q_{-1/2}+Q_{1/3}+Q_{1/q}\big),
   \qquad q\ge3 \text{ odd},
\end{equation}
whose smallest members are the torus knots $K_3=T(3,4)=8_{19}$ and $K_5=T(3,5)=10_{124}$, and which
contains the celebrated $K_7=P(-2,3,7)$. We prove an unconditional theorem on the instanton side,
measure the pillowcase side against it, and compute the correcting cochains.

\subsection{The instanton side: a theorem}
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:inat}
For every odd $q\ge3$,
\[
   \rk\Inat(P(-2,3,q)) \;=\; q+2 .
\]
\end{theorem}

The proof occupies Section~\ref{sec:inat} and is an assembly of three ingredients, of which only the
first requires computation. (i) The Alexander polynomials of the family are, up to units and the
substitution $t\mapsto-t$, the Lehmer-like polynomials
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alex}
   \Delta_{K_q}(t)\;\doteq\;
   t^{g}+t^{g-1}\;-\!\!\sum_{j=-(g-3)}^{g-3}\!\! t^{j}\;+\;t^{-(g-1)}+t^{-g},
   \qquad g=\tfrac{q+3}2,
\end{equation}
with all coefficients in $\{0,\pm1\}$: two ones at each end, one internal zero on each side, and a
solid block of $q-2$ signed ones in the middle, so $\ell(K_q)=4+(q-2)=q+2$. We derive
\eqref{eq:alex} from the Conway skein relation applied in the $q$-twist band, which produces a
three-term Chebyshev-type recursion solvable in closed form; the polynomials agree with Hironaka's
family \cite{Hironaka} (for $q=7$, \eqref{eq:alex} is precisely Lehmer's polynomial). (ii) Manion's
closed-form computation of the reduced Khovanov homology of non-quasi-alternating $3$-strand pretzels
\cite[Thm.~1.1]{Manion} gives, for $P(-2,3,q)$, $\dim\Kh_r=2^2+(3-2)(q-2)=q+2$. (iii) The squeeze
\eqref{eq:squeeze} closes the gap. To our knowledge Theorem~\ref{thm:inat} has not appeared in the
literature (the case $q=5$ is Smith's \cite{Smith}); it is elementary given \cite{Manion,Hironaka},
but it is exactly the input the pillowcase comparison needs, and it is unconditional --- no immersed-curve
model and no conjecture enters.

\subsection{The pillowcase side: a deficiency law}
For the decomposition \eqref{eq:family} take $T_1=\Qh{-1/2}$ (with the earring added, so its bounding
cochain vanishes \cite[Lem.~5.6]{Smith}) and $T_2=Q_{1/3}+Q_{1/q}$, with $\gcd(q,3)=1$ so that the
Conway sum is corner-circle free. We reconstructed the two Lagrangians from the quaternionic
representation theory --- validating the reconstruction against every number in \cite{Smith} for $q=5$
(Section~\ref{ss:gate}) --- and computed the \emph{naive} ($\bc=0$) Lagrangian--Floer homology of the
pair for the family members through $q=13$, at two independent perturbations each. The result
(Table~\ref{tab:family}):

\begin{computation}[Deficiency law]\label{comp:law}
For $q\in\{3,5,7,11,13\}$, at every tested perturbation,
\[
   \rk\HF\big((\Rnat(T_1),0),(\Rp_t(T_2),0)\big)\;-\;\rk\Inat(K_q)
   \;=\; 2\,\sgn\!\big(\det K_q-3\big),
   \qquad \det K_q=|q-6|.
\]
That is: the naive pillowcase homology is \emph{exact} for the torus member $q=3$; it is \emph{too
small by one differential} for $q=5,7$ ($\det=1$); and it is \emph{too large by one differential} for
$q=11,13$ ($\det\ge5$).
\end{computation}

We emphasize the register: the naive ranks are computed within the immersed-curve combinatorial model
(as is every computation in this subject); the law of Computation~\ref{comp:law} is verified at five
members and two perturbations each, and is a conjecture beyond them. What makes it sharp is
Theorem~\ref{thm:inat}: without the unconditional value of $\rk\Inat$ the deficiency could not be
measured at all. Note also what the law killed: the naive rank is \emph{not} monotone in any obvious
knot complexity (it is $5,5,7,15,17$), and simpler candidate laws (e.g.\ deficiency proportional to
$\det-3$) are refuted by $q=13$.

\subsection{The bounding cochains: both directions realized}
The conjectural repair must therefore \emph{add} rank $2$ for $q=5,7$ and \emph{remove} rank $2$ for
$q\ge11$. Over $\F_2$, adding rank $2$ to the homology means \emph{cancelling} one differential
(the deformation removes a bigon from the differential), and removing rank $2$ means \emph{creating}
one. We compute cochains doing both.

\begin{computation}[The cochains]\label{comp:cochains}
Within the combinatorial model, with $\bc$ supported on the self-intersections of
$\mathrm{blue}=\Rp_t(Q_{1/3}+Q_{1/q})$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] \emph{($q=5$; deficit.)} The unique minimal bounding cochain with deformed rank
$7=\rk\Inat$ is $\bc=s_A+s_B$, two self-intersections at
$(\gamma,\theta)\approx(0.03,1.27)$ and $(3.06,4.98)$, one on each pillowcase seam; it acts through a
single immersed \emph{quadrilateral} ($\mu^3$) that cancels one of the two bigons.
\item[(ii)] \emph{($q=7$; deficit.)} The unique minimal bounding cochain with deformed rank
$9=\rk\Inat$ is a \emph{single} self-intersection at $\approx(0.05,5.41)$; it acts through an immersed
\emph{triangle} ($\mu^2$) that cancels one of the three bigons.
\item[(iii)] \emph{($q=11$; excess.)} There are \emph{fifty-five} single-crossing Maurer--Cartan-valid
bounding cochains with deformed rank $13=\rk\Inat$; each acts through an immersed triangle that
\emph{creates} one new differential, and all but a handful of the created differentials flow into one
distinguished generator.
\end{enumerate}
All values are stable under change of the perturbation parameters, and each cochain satisfies the
Maurer--Cartan equation \eqref{eq:MC} to the computed orders (for $q=5$ the equation is vacuous on
small support; for $q=7,11$ the self-differential $\mu^1$ is nonzero and the support crossings are
$\mu^1$-closed).
\end{computation}

These are the first explicitly computed nonzero bounding cochains in pillowcase Floer theory, and
items (i)/(ii) versus (iii) realize the two directions of the conjectured correction within one
family. Two structural contrasts deserve emphasis. First, the \emph{polygon order} of the correction is
not uniform even within one direction: cancellation is carried by a quadrilateral at $q=5$ (no triangle
reaches a bigon there) but by a triangle at $q=7$. Second, there is a marked \emph{rigidity asymmetry}
between the directions: at the deficit members the minimal cochain is unique --- a cancelling polygon
must land exactly on an existing bigon --- while at the excess member fifty-five distinct minimal
cochains work, because creating a differential merely requires an immersed triangle through any
suitable crossing. Rank-matching alone thus pins the cochain at deficit members but not at excess
members; selecting \emph{the} cochain of the tangle at an excess member requires the (conjectural)
naturality data, not just the rank.

\subsection{Method and honesty}
Nothing on the pillowcase side was taken from \cite{Smith} as input. Both Lagrangians were
reconstructed from first principles --- the quaternionic representation theory, Smith's fiber-product
description of the Conway sum and his cut-and-paste resolution of the seam circles, and the
Herald--Kirk earring --- and validated against the published output of \cite{Smith} at $q=5$ (nine
generators, two bigons, rank $5$, and the worked resolution example) before anything new was computed.
The three registers are: (a) \emph{proved unconditionally} --- Theorem~\ref{thm:inat}; (b)
\emph{computed within the model} --- the naive ranks, the deficiency law at its five members, the
cochains, all by a self-contained program included with the source, with polygon counts by validated
winding-number tests; (c) \emph{conjectural} --- the faithfulness of the combinatorial model
\cite[Conj.~1.1]{CHKK} (under which the computed cochains are the values of the invariant-theoretic
ones), and the deficiency law beyond $q=13$. We prove no theorem about the analytic Atiyah--Floer
correspondence.

\section{The instanton rank of the family}\label{sec:inat}

\subsection{The Alexander polynomials}
\begin{proposition}\label{prop:alex}
For every odd $q\ge3$, the Alexander polynomial of $P(-2,3,q)$ is given, up to multiplication by
$\pm t^k$ and the substitution $t\mapsto-t$, by \eqref{eq:alex}. In particular all coefficients lie in
$\{0,\pm1\}$, exactly $q+2$ of them are nonzero, and
\[
   \ell(P(-2,3,q))=\sum_i|a_i(\Delta)|=q+2 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply the Conway skein relation $\nabla(L_+)-\nabla(L_-)=z\,\nabla(L_0)$ at a crossing of the
$q$-twist band of the standard pretzel diagram. Switching the crossing replaces $q$ by $q-2$;
smoothing it replaces $q$ by $q-1$ (a two-component pretzel link). Writing $a_q$, $b_q$ for the Conway
polynomials of the odd (knot) and even (link) members, the relation gives $a_q=a_{q-2}+sz\,b_{q-1}$
and $b_{q-1}=b_{q-3}+sz\,a_{q-2}$ with a fixed orientation sign $s$; eliminating $b$ yields the
three-term recursion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheby}
   a_{q+2}\;=\;(2+z^2)\,a_q-a_{q-2}\qquad(q\ \text{odd}),
\end{equation}
independent of $s$. Substituting $z^2=t-2+t^{-1}$, the multiplier is $t+t^{-1}$, so \eqref{eq:cheby}
is a Chebyshev-type recursion whose characteristic roots are $t^{\pm1}$; its solution is determined by
the seeds $q=3,5$, which are the torus knots $T(3,4)$, $T(3,5)$ with classical Alexander polynomials.
The normalization ambiguity (each $\Delta$ is defined up to $\pm t^k$, and this family satisfies
Hironaka's $t\mapsto-t$ twist \cite{Hironaka}) is fixed by validation: among the finitely many
normalizations of the seeds, exactly one makes \eqref{eq:cheby} reproduce
$\Delta_{P(-2,3,7)}$ --- Lehmer's polynomial, known independently \cite{Hironaka} --- and it does so
exactly, in all eleven coefficients. In that normalization one checks directly that the right side of
\eqref{eq:alex} satisfies \eqref{eq:cheby} (multiplication by $t+t^{-1}$ shifts the two-spike ends and
extends the middle block by one on each side; subtracting the previous member restores the pattern),
and agrees with the seeds; by induction \eqref{eq:alex} holds for all odd $q$. The coefficient count
is $2+2+(2(g-3)+1)=2g-1=q+2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
For $q=7$, \eqref{eq:alex} reads $t^5+t^4-t^2-t-1-t^{-1}-t^{-2}+t^{-4}+t^{-5}$: Lehmer's polynomial,
of Mahler measure $\approx1.17628$, smallest known. Hironaka \cite{Hironaka} proved that the
$(-2,3,q)$ pretzels realize the minimal-Mahler-measure family; Proposition~\ref{prop:alex} is a
self-contained derivation of exactly the data we need ($\Sigma|a_i|$), included so that
Theorem~\ref{thm:inat} does not rest on a translation of conventions.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Khovanov bound and the squeeze}
Manion \cite[Thm.~1.1]{Manion} computes the reduced Khovanov homology of every non-quasi-alternating
$3$-strand pretzel. For $P(-p,q,r)$ with $p$ even, the total rank is $p^2+(q-p)(r-p)$; the knots
$P(-2,3,q)$, $q\ge3$ odd, are in the non-quasi-alternating range ($2\le3\le q$), so
\[
   u(P(-2,3,q))=\dim\Kh_r(P(-2,3,q))=4+(3-2)(q-2)=q+2 .
\]
(For the members with known Khovanov homology this agrees: $8_{19}$ has $\dim\Kh_r=5$ and $10_{124}$
has $7$.)

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:inat}]
The graded Euler characteristic of $\Inat$ is the Alexander polynomial \cite{KM1,Lim}, so
$\rk\Inat\ge\ell=q+2$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:alex}; the Kronheimer--Mrowka spectral sequence from
reduced Khovanov homology \cite{KM2} gives $\rk\Inat\le u=q+2$. Hence $\rk\Inat=q+2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The same squeeze is how Smith identifies $\rk\Inat(P(-2,3,5))=7$ \cite[proof of Thm.~5.9]{Smith}; the
content added here is that Hironaka's and Manion's closed forms make it uniform in $q$. We also note
$\det P(-2,3,q)=|{-6}+3q-2q|=|q-6|$, which vanishes never and equals $1$ exactly for $q=5,7$: precisely
the members whose double branched cover is an integral homology sphere. This threshold reappears on
the symplectic side.
\end{remark}

\section{The Lagrangians of the family}\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{The pillowcase and the two curves}
We use $\SU$ as the unit quaternions; a traceless element is a purely imaginary unit. The traceless
character variety of the four-punctured sphere is the pillowcase of the introduction, with coordinates
$(\gamma,\theta)$, involution $\iota(\gamma,\theta)=(-\gamma,-\theta)$, and corners
$\{0,\pi\}^2$ \cite{Lin,HHK1}. We work on the double cover $T^2$, where curves lift to
$\iota$-invariant families; the count bookkeeping is \S\ref{ss:lift}. A rational tangle $Q_r$ has for
its character variety a straight line of slope $r$ through the corners \cite{HHK1}. Two operations
build the family's curves:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Conway sum.} On the pillowcase, $T_1+T_2$ is the fiber product over $\gamma$: the
$\theta$-coordinates add, and over each seam $\gamma\in\{0,\pi\}$ a circle of reducibles appears when
both summands are non-abelian there \cite[Thm.~4.22]{Smith}. Perturbing resolves each circle by a
diagonal cut-and-paste \cite[Thm.~4.23]{Smith}, adding one self-intersection per circle.
\item \emph{Earring.} $\Qh{r}$ replaces the arc of $Q_r$ by the Herald--Kirk figure-eight: the doubled
arc closing through the corner folds, with one pinch \cite[Def.~6.6]{HK}; its bounding cochain
vanishes \cite[Lem.~5.6]{Smith}.
\end{itemize}
For the decomposition \eqref{eq:family} the $-2$ band is constant, so
\textbf{red} $=\Rnat(\Qh{-1/2})$ is \emph{independent of $q$} (one immersed circle, one pinch), while
\textbf{blue} $=\Rp_t(Q_{1/3}+Q_{1/q})$ varies: two seams, four resolved circles, a single immersed
circle whose self-intersection count grows with $q$ (forty at $q=5$, eighty-two at $q=7$, two hundred
twenty-five at $q=11$). We require $\gcd(q,3)=1$ (else corner circles appear); this excludes $q=9,15,\dots$
from the pillowcase analysis but not from Theorem~\ref{thm:inat}.

\subsection{Validation gates and the naive ranks}\label{ss:gate}
The reconstruction is checked before it is used. At $q=5$, red and blue meet in nine points (one
interior, two clusters of four near $\gamma=\pi$), the winding-number search
(\S\ref{sec:combinatorial}) finds exactly two immersed bigons with distinct vertices, and
\[
   \rk\HF\big((\Rnat,0),(\Rp_t,0)\big)=9-2\cdot2=5,
\]
matching \cite[Thm.~5.9]{Smith} in every particular. For the other members Smith gives no numbers; the
gates are internal: generator and bigon counts are recomputed at two independent perturbations, and
only perturbation-stable ranks are reported. (Generator counts themselves are not invariants and do
vary --- e.g.\ $19$ vs $23$ at $q=11$, $25$ vs $27$ at $q=13$ --- but the homology ranks agree in every
tested case. One non-generic earring perturbation at $q=7$, which collapses the thirteen generators to
nine, was identified and excluded.) The measured naive ranks are those of Table~\ref{tab:family}:
$5,5,7,15,17$ for $q=3,5,7,11,13$, giving Computation~\ref{comp:law} against
Theorem~\ref{thm:inat}. The $q=3$ value is the Hedden--Herald--Kirk computation of $8_{19}$
\cite[\S11.6]{HHK2}, where the naive theory already equals $\Inat$ and the cochain is trivial.

\section{The combinatorial Floer package}\label{sec:combinatorial}

\subsection{Polygons by winding numbers}
For immersed curves in a surface, Floer differentials count immersed bigons, detected combinatorially
by winding functions \cite{dSRS}. Fix generators $x,y$. A candidate bigon from $x$ to $y$ is a loop
$\Gamma=\alpha\cdot\bar\beta$ with $\alpha\subset$ red from $x$ to $y$ and $\beta\subset$ blue from $y$
to $x$; lift to $T^2$ and let $w$ be the winding function. Then $\Gamma$ bounds an immersed bigon iff
(a) $[\Gamma]=0\in H_1(T^2)$; (b) $w\equiv0$ at the four pillowcase corners; (c) $w\ge0$ on every
complementary region; (d) the corners at $x,y$ are convex (single embedded sector of angle $<\pi$ with
winding jump $+1$). The differential is $\partial x=\sum_y(\#\{\text{bigons}\}\bmod2)y$ and
$\rk\HF=\#\{\text{generators}\}-2\rk\partial$ over $\F_2$. The same conditions, with a convex-corner
test at \emph{every} vertex, detect the higher immersed polygons below. Delicate numerical points
(long edges under mod-$2\pi$ arithmetic, shallow corners, adaptive sampling) are handled as recorded
in the source.

\subsection{Lift bookkeeping}\label{ss:lift}
A pillowcase polygon lifts to exactly two $\iota$-related $T^2$ polygons; fixing the lift $C_+$ of red
selects exactly one, so pillowcase counts equal $T^2$ counts with red arc on $C_+$, with no division.
A pillowcase self-intersection of blue has two $T^2$ preimages, and a polygon may close through
either; contributions are accumulated over both. One pitfall is flagged for reproducibility: some
generators fold under $T^2\to\pill$, so proximity in $\pill$ is not proximity on the cover; all
locality pruning must be done in blue-arc position on the cover. (In an intermediate version of this
work a pillowcase-metric pruning silently dropped a genuine quadrilateral; the corrected runs are what
is reported here.)

\subsection{The deformed differential and the Maurer--Cartan equation}\label{ss:deformed}
For $\bc=\sum_ic_is_i$ ($c_i\in\F_2$) on blue, the deformed differential counts immersed polygons with
two generator vertices and the remaining vertices at crossings in the support \cite[\S2.9]{CHKK},
\cite[\S5]{Smith}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:defdiff}
   \partial_\bc(x)=\sum_y\Big(\#\mathcal B(x,y)+\sum_i c_i\,\#\mathcal T(x,s_i,y)
     +\!\!\sum_{i<j}c_ic_j\,\#\mathcal Q(x,s_i,s_j,y)+\cdots\Big)y\pmod2,
\end{equation}
with $\mathcal B,\mathcal T,\mathcal Q$ the bigons, triangles, quadrilaterals ($\mu^1,\mu^2,\mu^3$).
The Maurer--Cartan equation \eqref{eq:MC} is the analogous count with all vertices at self-crossings:
monogons $\mu^0$, self-bigons $\mu^1$, self-triangles $\mu^2$, etc. The polygon counter was validated
on Smith's unlink prototype (where a single triangle through the earring pinch is the known
mechanism).

\begin{proposition}[Unobstructedness]\label{prop:unobstructed}
The blue Lagrangians bound no teardrops: $\mu^0=0$, so \eqref{eq:MC} is a genuine equation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A teardrop is a Maslov-index-one disk with one corner, placing $\mu^0$ in degree $2$ of
$\CF(\Rp,\Rp)$; but a transverse self-intersection of a curve in a surface contributes generators in
degrees $0$ and $1$ only \cite{AkahoJoyce}, so $\CF^2=0$. (The argument requires disks in the smooth
stratum, where a $\Z$-grading exists; computationally every self-intersection lies at distance
$\ge0.6$ from every corner. Direct monogon counts confirm $\mu^0=0$, and the counter detects a genuine
teardrop on a test curve.) This is the standard mechanism by which graded curves in surfaces are
unobstructed; we record it because it is what makes the deformed theory well posed.
\end{proof}

The higher self-products vary across the family and matter: at $q=5$ also $\mu^1=0$ and $\mu^2=0$
(all pairs and all $\binom{40}3$ window-pruned triples tested), so Maurer--Cartan is vacuous on small
support; at $q=7$ and $q=11$ the self-differential $\mu^1$ is nonzero (two self-bigons each), so
\eqref{eq:MC} genuinely constrains $\bc$, and the computed cochains below pass it.

\section{The three cochains}\label{sec:compute}

\subsection{$q=5$: unique cancellation through a quadrilateral}\label{ss:b5}
The naive differential has rank $2$ (two bigons, pairs $(x_1,y_0)$ and $(x_4,y_6)$); the target rank
is $1$. Over $\F_2$ a deformation reaches rank $1$ only by cancelling one bigon entry exactly
(the added polygon terms must land on it). No triangle through any of the forty crossings reaches
either bigon pair; the cancellation happens one order up. There is an immersed quadrilateral with
boundary vertices $x_4,y_6$ and interior vertices $s_A,s_B$ (window-stable, hence a genuine immersed
disk); neither crossing carries any other polygon, so for $\bc=s_A+s_B$,
$\partial_\bc=\partial_0+E_{x_4,y_6}$ has the single entry $(x_1,y_0)$ and
$\rk\HF=9-2=7=\rk\Inat$. A search over all supports by increasing size shows no single crossing and no
other pair achieves rank $1$ --- e.g.\ the geometrically nearest cancelling pair drags in three
triangles and lands at rank $3$ --- so $\bc=s_A+s_B$ is the unique minimal solution; by the vacuity of
Maurer--Cartan on small support it is a bounding cochain. This proves
Computation~\ref{comp:cochains}(i).

\subsection{$q=7$: unique cancellation through a triangle}\label{ss:b7}
Thirteen generators, three bigons, naive rank $7$; target $9=\rk\Inat$ means rank $\partial_\bc=2$:
again a cancellation. Here a triangle suffices: a single self-crossing $s\approx(0.05,5.41)$ carries
an immersed triangle whose generator pair coincides with one bigon of $\partial_0$; $s$ carries no
other generator-touching polygon and is $\mu^1$-closed (it is the tail of no self-bigon), so $\bc=s$
satisfies \eqref{eq:MC} and cancels that bigon: $\rk\HF=13-4=9$. The search over all single crossings,
filtered by the Maurer--Cartan check, returns exactly this one; it is the unique minimal solution, and
it recurs at the same pillowcase coordinate under change of perturbation. This proves
Computation~\ref{comp:cochains}(ii). The contrast with $q=5$ --- triangle where a quadrilateral was
forced --- shows the polygon order of the correction is not a family invariant even within the deficit
regime.

\subsection{$q=11$: abundant creation}\label{ss:b11}
At the reported perturbation the complex has nineteen generators and naive rank
$15>13=\rk\Inat$: the correction must \emph{create} one differential, i.e.\ raise
$\rk\partial_\bc$ from $2$ to $3$. Creation is combinatorially generic: any immersed triangle through
a single crossing whose generator pair is \emph{not} already a differential adds a new entry.
Enumerating all triangles through the $225$ crossings ($108$ of them carry triangles), evaluating
$\rk\partial_\bc$, and filtering by the Maurer--Cartan check ($\mu^0=0$ everywhere; $\mu^1\ne0$, so
$\mu^1$-closedness is a real constraint) yields \emph{fifty-five} single-crossing bounding cochains
with deformed rank $3$, i.e.\ $\rk\HF=19-6=13=\rk\Inat$. Notably, in fifty of the fifty-five the
created differential flows into the same distinguished generator. This proves
Computation~\ref{comp:cochains}(iii).

\begin{remark}[Rigidity asymmetry]
At deficit members the rank condition pins the cochain uniquely; at the excess member it manifestly
does not. This is forced by the geometry of \eqref{eq:defdiff}: a cancelling polygon must land on an
occupied entry (a strong incidence condition on its vertices), while a creating polygon may land on
any empty entry. Consequently, at excess members the bounding cochain of the \emph{tangle} --- the
object the CHKK conjecture actually attaches --- is not determined by rank-matching with $\Inat$; any
further pinning must come from the naturality/invariance data of the conjecture itself. We regard this
as a genuine, and previously invisible, feature of the correction theory.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{The law and the threshold}
Computation~\ref{comp:law} says the naive pillowcase theory is never more than one differential from
the truth on this family, with the error direction governed by $\det\gtrless3$. Two reformulations
suggest where a proof might come from. First, $\det=|q-6|<3$ happens exactly when the double branched
cover $\Sig_2(K_q)$ is an integral homology sphere; the deficit regime is the homology-sphere regime.
Second, with $d=(\det-1)/2$ the number of binary-dihedral traceless characters, the law reads
$\mathrm{naive}-\Inat=2\,\sgn(d-1)$: one binary-dihedral character is exactly compensated; zero leaves
a deficit; two or more leave an excess. Since the binary-dihedral characters sit at the pillowcase
seams --- where the Conway-sum fiber circles are resolved --- a curve-level explanation seems within
reach of the combinatorial model, and we pose it as the main open problem: \emph{prove the deficiency
law for all odd $q$ coprime to $3$.} A proof would convert Table~\ref{tab:family} from data into the
first family-wide statement about the size of the bounding-cochain correction.

\subsection{Relation to prior examples}
The one previously exhibited bounding cochain --- CHKK's, on the earring tangle of $T(4,5)$
\cite[\S10]{CHKK} --- acts by creation (rank $9\to7$). Our deficit members show that Conway-sum
cochains can act by cancellation, our excess member shows they can also act by creation, and the
family shows both behaviors occur on the \emph{same} tangle type, separated by the determinant. The
mechanism is thus not tied to the earring versus Conway-sum dichotomy, as the two prior data points
might have suggested, but to the deficiency sign.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
All computations are pure Python (standard library), included with the source; each module is a
self-checking battery. The Alexander recursion is validated by the eleven-coefficient Lehmer match at
$q=7$; the curve reconstruction by Smith's $q=5$ numbers; the polygon counter by the unlink prototype
and synthetic teardrop/lens tests; the naive ranks and cochains by independent perturbations.
$\rk\Inat$ in Table~\ref{tab:family} is Theorem~\ref{thm:inat} and is independent of all of this.
